New York City Flood Hazards

Past, Present and Future Risks

New York City is a densely populated and low lying coastal city of 8.4 million people. Consisting of 5 boroughs spread across peninsulas and islands, NYC is connected through a vast infrastructure network of bridges, ferries, and tunnels. The low lying coastal topography and percentage of New York City covered with impervious surfaces (sidewalks, roads, dense built environment) increase the current risks of serious flooding. Furthermore, the future potential for weather related flooding events is predicted to increase with an upwards trend in more severe climate related weather events and the inevitable sea level rise as a result of climate change


The Federal Emergency Managment Agency (FEMA) identifies Special Flood Hazard Areas (SFHAs) by determining the likelihood that a certain area will be innundated by water in a given year. While there are many different classifications and subclassficiations, the most common SFHAs are the "100 year flood zone" and the "500 year flood zone". In coastal areas like NYC, there are also special wave hazard areas that are significant beacause of the addtional dangers associated with strong storm surge waves.
